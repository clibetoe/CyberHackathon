The CIA Triad: The Foundation of Security

Information security is built upon three core principles known as the CIA Triad. These principles guide the development of security systems, standards, and policies to ensure that information is protected at all stages.

Confidentiality

Ensures that information is not disclosed to unauthorized individuals, entities, or processes. It's about keeping secrets safe.

Integrity

Maintains the consistency, accuracy, and trustworthiness of data over its entire lifecycle. Data must not be changed in transit or altered by unauthorized people.

Availability

Guarantees that information and systems are accessible and usable upon demand by an authorized user. It protects against downtime and data loss.

The Balancing Act

A perfectly secure system that is unusable is worthless. Effective information security requires a delicate balance between security, functionality, and usability. Increasing one often comes at the expense of the others, and the ideal mix depends on the system's purpose and its users.

Defense in Depth: A Layered Approach

This strategy implements multiple, overlapping security controls. If one layer of defense is breached, another is already in place to thwart the attack. This creates redundancy and significantly reduces the overall risk.

Essential Security Terminology

Vulnerability

A weakness or flaw in a system that can be exploited.

Threat

Anything that has the potential to violate system security.

Exploit

The mechanism used to take advantage of a vulnerability.

Payload

The part of an exploit that causes damage or steals data.

Zero-day Attack

An attack targeting a vulnerability unknown to the vendor.

Daisy Chaining

Using one successful attack to launch another.

Types of Security Controls

Controls by Function

These controls are defined by their purpose in the security lifecycle, from preventing threats to recovering from them.

  • Preventive: Stop threats from happening (e.g., fences, authentication).
  • Detective: Detect threats that have occurred (e.g., audits, cameras).
  • Deterrent: Discourage potential attackers (e.g., warning signs, guards).
  • Mitigating/Recovery: Minimize impact and restore operations (e.g., system isolation).
  • Corrective/Compensating: Fix or cover for known vulnerabilities (e.g., network isolation).

A comprehensive security strategy integrates multiple types of controls across all layers of defense to protect information effectively.
