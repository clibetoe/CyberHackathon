⛓️ The Cyber Kill Chain

A security model outlining the seven distinct phases of a cyberattack, from initial reconnaissance to the attacker's final objective.

The Seven Stages of an Attack

The Cyber Kill Chain provides a framework to understand and defend against advanced cyber threats. Each stage represents a step an attacker must complete to achieve their goal.

1

Reconnaissance

Gathering information about the target. This can be passive (footprinting) or active (direct interaction like port scanning).

2

Weaponization

Creating a malicious deliverable, like a phishing email with a malicious attachment, to conduct the attack.

3

Delivery

Transmitting the weaponized payload to the victim's environment, often via email, USB drives, or physical access.

4

Exploitation

Executing the code to take advantage of a vulnerability on the target system. This is where the attack begins.

5

Installation

Installing the primary malware, backdoor, or remote access tool on the target system to ensure persistent access.

6

Command and Control (C2)

The attacker establishes a communication channel to control the compromised system and move laterally within the network.

7

Actions on Objectives

The final stage where the attacker achieves their initial goals, such as data exfiltration or system destruction.

Detection Difficulty by Stage

While defenders can implement controls at every stage, the difficulty of detecting a threat varies greatly. The chart below shows how active interaction with a target (like reconnaissance) is easier to detect than more subtle, internal actions.

Why Detection Varies

1. Reconnaissance

Detection is difficult during passive recon (no direct interaction), but active scanning leaves a clear digital footprint that can be easily detected by security tools.

2. Weaponization

This stage happens offline and is not detectable by the target, as the attacker is simply creating the malicious payload.

3. Delivery

This is a key chokepoint. While some methods are stealthy, others, like phishing emails, are often flagged by mail filters or antivirus software.

4. Exploitation

The initial exploit often uses a small, stealthy "dropper" payload, making it difficult to spot within the noise of network traffic.

5. Installation

This is one of the hardest stages to detect. Malware is designed to mimic legitimate processes and hide deep within the system, often evading standard security tools.

6. Command & Control (C2)

While stealthy, this stage creates network traffic that can be identified by sophisticated Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) looking for unusual communication patterns.

7. Actions on Objectives

This is the most detectable stage. Actions like data exfiltration or system destruction generate massive network and system activity, which security teams are trained to identify.

Understanding the Cyber Kill Chain allows security teams to create a more effective defense by focusing on interrupting the attack at its earliest possible stage.
